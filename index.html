Before We Begin

What are we about to show?

Every interaction between particles in nature — light bouncing off your hand, a nucleus decaying, a proton staying bound together — is one of just three fundamental forces at the particle level (gravity aside). Below is a short explanation of each force, followed by what a Feynman diagram actually is, before we open the full interactive illustration.

Force 1

Electromagnetic Interaction

Acts between any electrically charged particles — electrons, protons, ions — by exchanging a photon (γ), the particle of light itself.

  • Carrier: Photon — massless
  • Range: Infinite
  • Powers: Light, electricity, magnetism, chemistry, atomic structure
Force 2

Weak Interaction

Responsible for radioactive decay and the nuclear fusion that powers stars. Carried by the heavy W± and Z⁰ bosons.

  • Carrier: W±, Z⁰ — very massive
  • Range: Extremely short (~10⁻¹⁸ m)
  • Special: The only force that changes one particle into another (e.g. neutron → proton)
Force 3

Strong Interaction

Binds quarks together inside protons and neutrons, and holds the nucleus together, via the exchange of gluons (g).

  • Carrier: Gluon — massless but self-interacting
  • Range: Extremely short, grows stronger with distance
  • Special: Gluons carry colour charge, so they can interact with each other
The Tool We Use To Picture This

What is a Feynman Diagram?

A shorthand map of a particle interaction

Invented by physicist Richard Feynman, it is not a photograph of what happens in space — it is a picture-based accounting system for particle physics. Every line and vertex represents a real, physical quantity used to calculate the interaction.

Time axis:Reads left → right; incoming particles enter left, outgoing particles exit right.
Straight lines:Fermions — matter particles (electrons, quarks, neutrinos). The arrow shows particle-flow direction.
Wavy / curly lines:Force carriers — photon, W/Z boson, or gluon — being exchanged.
Each dot (vertex):A real interaction point, governed by a coupling constant.
